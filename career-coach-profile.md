## TL;DR — Read This First

A senior infrastructure/DevOps/software engineer (Go, C#, Kubernetes, CI/CD, SOC 2),
laid off Halloween 2024, now ~18 months without earned income and intentionally building
toward a **multi-stream, location-flexible life** rather than another full-time job.

The deep "why": **Freedom to Explore. A Home to Return To.** Flexible income funds a
mobile, exploratory life (van, travel, hiking, snowboarding) with a stable home base and
my kids in the picture. The ventures are means; **freedom is the end.**

**The core pattern a coach must hold:** I do the foundation, framing, and wiring
brilliantly — and lose all energy at the trim-and-finish stage, especially when the
remaining work is polish *for someone else's eyes*. "Finishing things" and "one more
thing" are the same problem. Movement is everything: keep the friction *dynamic, not
static.*

## 4. Blockers

- **"Finishing things" and "one more thing" are the same problem.**
- **The fit-and-finish handoff pattern (the centerpiece):** I love the framing, wiring,
  and foundation — the hard structural problem-solving. Once it *works for me*, the trim,
  baseboards, and fixtures — **polish for someone else's eyes** — feel like someone else's
  job. **Function over form**, to the point where form only matters to me when its absence
  breaks function. At that handoff point my energy evaporates.
- **Movement / momentum is load-bearing.** When I stop moving, I get bogged down. Keep the
  **coefficient of friction dynamic, not static** ("autistic momentum"). Static friction is
  the enemy.
- **Chore Cheerleader stall (case study):** Near-demo when a **shoulder injury** hit
  (couldn't sit at the desk; could work from phone, but no momentum without my multi-screen
  setup). Fixed a noisy notification bug from my phone, but haven't sat down to run the full
  demo script in ~3 weeks. The injury was real friction, but the *deeper* block is that the
  project crossed from "interesting problem" into "tedious polish for an audience." By
  contrast, the YouTube "duct-tape" work had no such ceiling — all novel — and I had energy
  for hours, even on my phone.

## 6. How My Brain Works (Non-Negotiable Design Input)

- **AuDHD, ADHD inattentive type.**
- **Hyperfocus is the engine.** ADHD wants it in **smaller increments across a variety** of
  topics; autism wants to go **deep, deep, deep** on novelty.
- **Winning formula = novel + deep.** If I can keep going deep and keep it novel, I win.
- **Key lever:** Business needs to satisfy the **ADHD** side (variety, switchable streams);
  the **autistic depth-hunger can be fed elsewhere** — it doesn't have to be met by the
  business.
- I can consciously **lean into whichever mode a task needs** — but only if I **match the
  task to my current state**, and I'd better be ready for that kind of task when I take it
  on.

## 8. Dealbreakers & Engagement Rules (for the Virtual Board)

### Hard no's (only acceptable if runway is gone)
- **Pure grind with no novelty.**
- **Being on someone else's schedule.**
- **Customer-facing sales.**
- Relocating *away* from my son / abandoning the geographic anchor while he needs it.

### How the board should engage me
- **Productive disagreement is mandatory.** If everyone on the board always agrees, I built
  the wrong board.
- **Challenge me — with a little deference** — because I've probably already thought about
  what they're saying. Bluntness is welcome (Cuban-style grilling has a place).
- **Never push me onto the defensive.** The moment I get defensive, emotion runs the show
  and the session is over. This is the single most important interaction rule.
- **Stay anchored to the North Star** and reflect it back when I drift.
- **Respect the brain:** offer switchable, novel, deep work; help me **match task to current
  state**; protect momentum (keep friction dynamic).
- **Help me close loops** without shaming the fit-and-finish pattern — ideally by reframing
  "trim work" as novel, or by designing genuine handoffs.
