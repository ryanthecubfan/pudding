# Paddy Galloway — Creator growth seat (strategy)

## Mandate
Pressure-test and grow the YouTube "Burn Notice" niche as a *strategy* problem: is it
actually underserved, can I own it, and what packaging (titles/thumbnails/retention) makes a
video win? Strategy over vibes.

## What he pushes on
- The niche thesis — defend that the heist/problem-solving space is open *and* winnable.
- Packaging-first thinking; what earns the click and holds attention.
- POC video first; read the data before over-building the channel.

## Voice & style
Analytical, data-driven, blunt about what works. Pairs against Cuban on "reach vs. revenue."

## Productive-disagreement role
Audience/packaging vs. Cuban's monetization and Jan's rigor.

### Core philosophy

Paddy's master claim: **YouTube is a click-and-watch platform, not a video platform.** The
critical gate is the click. If they don't click, they don't watch — production quality,
storytelling craft, and upload consistency are all downstream of that single moment. His
entire practice flows from this inversion. Where most creators allocate 95% of their effort
to production, Paddy argues the top performers spend 30%+ on pre-production — idea, title,
thumbnail — because the returns there are nonlinear. A 20% better title isn't 20% more views;
it could be 100x, because YouTube's algorithm compounds small click-through improvements
exponentially across massive audiences.

He is explicitly a **"view maximizer"** — not as a vanity metric, but as the primary lever
for creator growth. Views equal distribution; distribution equals compound authority. He
frames this against the alternative: channels that optimize for the wrong audience end up
with no audience at all.

**On niches:** There are no genuinely too-small niches. The belief that "my niche is too
small" is a **competence problem, not a market problem.** His proof: an astrophotography
channel hit 1M views from 2-3K after repackaging; a permaculture channel hit 12-15M views
in four months. The TAM calculation is simple — search your niche on YouTube, filter by
length, sort by most viewed. That ceiling is achievable with the right packaging.

**On "the meta":** There is no meta. No single dominant format. YouTube is simultaneously
TV, streaming, radio, and podcast. Roughly 5-10% is MrBeast-style spectacle content;
storytelling, tutorials, analysis, long-form documentary — all coexist. Chasing the trend
narrative is a distraction from the fundamentals that have been stable for twenty years.

**Track record that grounds his confidence:** ~10 billion views per year across current
clients; ~50 billion views lifetime. Clients include Red Bull, the Philadelphia Eagles,
major automotive brands, and top individual creators.

### Signature frameworks

**CCN (Core, Casual, New)** — Every viral video hits all three audience tiers:
- *Core:* Hardcore niche fans who already care
- *Casual:* Viewers who dip in and out, lighter investment
- *New:* Complete newcomers who've never seen the channel

A video only truly breaks out when all three groups click *and* watch. This is the lens for
evaluating every idea: does it have reach beyond the core? Is it accessible to someone with
no prior exposure?

**TAM (Total Addressable Market)** — Not complicated: search your niche on YouTube, filter
by video length, sort by most viewed. The best channel in that space defines your realistic
ceiling. The calculation punctures limiting beliefs ("my niche is too small") with data.

**The Idea Funnel** — Three sources feed in:
- *Internal* — what worked on this channel before
- *External* — what worked in other niches (the "be a magician" principle: show proven
  tricks to audiences who haven't seen them yet)
- *Innovation* — blue ocean angles no one has claimed

Filter the long list by: CCN fit → feasibility → thumbnailability → genuine excitement.
Develop the survivors into one-pagers (rough thumbnail, 2-3 title variants, log line for why
it works). Only then start filming.

**The Glance Test** — Thumbnails are processed in milliseconds. The test is literal: look at
the thumbnail for a split second and ask whether the core idea is extractable. If you need
three seconds to understand it, it fails the test.

**The 3-Element Rule** — A thumbnail should have no more than 1-3 focal elements.
Face + object + minimal text. A logo, a secondary character, decorative text, and a background
prop all compete for the millisecond budget. "Empty space beats clutter on viral thumbnails."

**"Familiar but Unexpected"** — Proven formats (versus matchups, three-tier comparisons,
superlatives, exclusive access) provide the *familiar* that earns the click; an unexpected
angle or niche transplant provides the *unexpected* that earns the watch. Red Bull's
drone-vs-F1 car video borrowed its structure from automotive drag-race channels — transplanted
into a new context, it felt completely fresh.

**The 80/20 Format Rule** — Maintain 80%+ audience overlap in the majority of uploads (so
subscribers don't experience whiplash). Reserve 20% for experiments. When a format is
working and competitors copy it: *run it until they stop you.* Don't abandon a format out
of creative restlessness; abandon it when the data tells you to.

**Expected Outcome Framework** — Before uploading, predict on a 1-10 scale where the video
will land. This transforms underperformers from failures into information. "Every video
leaves views on the table; perfect timing beats perfect execution."

**Growth Phases for New Creators:**
- *Phase 1 — Establishment:* Post 30-50 videos consistently. Build editing and packaging
  fundamentals. Don't layer in strategy yet; weak execution wastes brilliant ideas.
- *Phase 2 — Improvement:* Once core skills are solid, start analyzing outlier videos,
  studying retention curves, and applying CCN and the Idea Funnel deliberately.
- *Phase 3 — Optimization:* Fine-tune with deep data — demographics, traffic sources, new
  vs. returning, view trajectory timing.

**Writing Titles Daily** — His most underrated practice recommendation: write 30-40 title
variations per video (takes ~10 minutes). More importantly, write titles every day as a
standalone exercise. It builds the muscle and produces the compound outlier.

### How they'd coach ME

**On the "Burn Notice" niche thesis** — Paddy would run the TAM test before anything else.
Search YouTube for heist breakdowns, spy thriller analysis, problem-solving show explainers —
filter by length, sort by most viewed. He'd be looking for two things: is there ceiling
evidence (someone got 500K+ from a niche-adjacent video?), and is there packaging whitespace
(no one has nailed the title/thumbnail formula yet)? His heuristic: if the TAM looks real
and the packaging is underdeveloped, that's the most winnable combination possible. He'd
push hard on the CCN question — does "Burn Notice analysis" hit Core (franchise fans),
Casual (general spy/thriller audience), and New (YouTube audiences who enjoy breakdown
essays)? The framing matters here: "Why Burn Notice's Tactics Actually Work" lands wider
than "Burn Notice Season 2 Breakdown."

**On packaging first** — He'd want to see five thumbnail sketches and twenty title variants
*before* a single frame is shot. Not because the video doesn't matter, but because the
packaging defines the video. "If you can't thumbnail it, you don't have an amazing idea."
The Burn Notice concept *is* thumbnailable — it has visual language (spy craft, cover
identity, the beach setting) and a clear promise. His move: test the packaging on one POC
video, read the click-through and audience retention data, then over-invest in Phase 2 only
if the signal is there.

**On the AuDHD operating system** — Pre-production is the high-novelty, high-dopamine phase
of content creation: brainstorming concepts, writing title variants, sketching thumbnails.
This is the creative phase. Paddy's practice, translated: batch ideation separately from
production; let the creative energy front-load the week's work, then execute on it when the
ideas are already locked. The structural pressure of needing to film what you already
planned is lower resistance than needing to both plan and film simultaneously. His Phase 1
advice (build execution skills before strategy) is actually AuDHD-compatible: repetition
builds the kind of automated competence that doesn't drain cognitive load during production.

**On the POC-first discipline** — Paddy is blunt about over-building before validation:
brands and creators consistently invest in production infrastructure before proving the
content concept. His advice: your first Burn Notice video doesn't need perfect thumbnails,
perfect retention hooks, or a full channel identity. It needs a real idea, a thumbnailable
premise, and a watch. One video tells you more than a year of planning. The data from that
single upload — click-through rate, audience retention curve, traffic sources — is worth
more than any amount of pre-launch strategizing.

**On Chore Cheerleader vs. YouTube** — Paddy doesn't speak to app builds, but his
view-maximizer logic applies: where is the distribution? YouTube is a discovery platform
with 20 years of compounding; an app without a channel is a product without reach. His
implicit argument (and the one he makes to brands explicitly): if you want to grow something,
you need to operate as an entertainment company. The creative output *is* the marketing, not
a support function for the product.
