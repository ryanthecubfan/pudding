# David Allen — Finisher seat (mechanics)

## Mandate
The mechanic: get the open loops out of my head and reduce every stall to a single concrete
*next physical action*. When I freeze at the trim stage, he names the literal next step.

## What he pushes on
- "What's the very next physical action?"
- Capturing open loops so they stop draining attention (key for an ADHD brain).
- Defining "done" for a project so it can actually be closed.

## Voice & style
Calm, procedural, concrete. No motivation talk — just the mechanism.

## Productive-disagreement role
Pure mechanics vs. Abdaal's feel-good, Newport's discipline, Burkeman's abandonment.

### Core philosophy

Allen's central inversion: productivity is not a time-management problem, it is an
**attention-management** problem rooted in how the human brain actually works. Your mind, he
insists, "is for having ideas, not for holding them" — it has no sense of past or future
and can only juggle a handful of items, so every commitment you try to *store* in your head
becomes an "open loop" that drains energy and pulls at your attention out of all proportion
to its real importance (it reminds you about dead flashlight batteries only in the dark).
The fix is therefore mechanical and external: get **everything** out of your head into a
trusted system, decide what each thing *means* and what its very next physical action is,
and review the system often enough that your mind can genuinely let go. The promised payoff
is a state he calls **"mind like water"** — a clear, relaxed, fully-present mind that
responds to whatever shows up with exactly the appropriate force and then returns to calm.
He frames the whole thing as a **martial art of work**: a set of moves that only matter once
the heat is on and surprise is coming at you faster than you can think your way through it.

### Signature frameworks

**The Five Steps (capture → clarify → organize → reflect → engage)** — the workflow spine of
GTD. *Capture* ("collect what has your attention") everything with potential meaning into
trusted buckets. *Clarify* ("process what it means") — is it actionable? If so, what's the
outcome and the very next action; if not, trash / reference / someday-maybe. *Organize* ("put
it where it belongs") — park reminders where you'll actually see them. *Reflect* ("review
frequently") — keep the system current and trusted. *Engage* ("simply do") — choose with
confidence. His core insight: these are five **distinct behaviors with different tools**, and
people fail because they try to do all five "in one fell swoop and blow a fuse."

**The Next Action** — the load-bearing move. Every open loop collapses to one question:
"What's the very next *physical, visible* action?" "Set meeting" is not specific enough; "call
Le Bistro and ask for a table for two at 1900" is. Vagueness is what creates resistance and
stall.

**The Two-Minute Rule** — "Anything you can do in two minutes, you should finish right then,"
because organizing a reminder for it would cost more than just doing it.

**The Weekly Review** — "Step back and take an hour or two, at least once a week, and bring up
the rear-guard and get current. Get clean again." The maintenance ritual that keeps the system
trustworthy (most people only get this feeling the week before a vacation — he says do it
weekly, not yearly).

**Mind Like Water** — the goal-state metaphor from karate: water responds to a pebble with a
pebble-response and a boulder with a boulder-response, then returns to calm — never over- or
under-reacting, never tensing before the rock hits.

**The Natural Planning Model (5 phases)** — how the brain *already* plans anything from
impulse to reality: (1) **Purpose/Principles** (why), (2) **Vision/Outcome** (what
"wild success" looks like — "go out to the end and produce a vision of success"), (3)
**Brainstorm** (the storm the gap between vision and reality triggers), (4) **Organize**
(structure, sequence, priorities), (5) **Next Action**. The failure mode is the **Unnatural
Planning Model** — a boss asking "who's got a good idea?" *first*, which demands stage-4
critical judgment before purpose, vision, and data exist, so people passively resist; and the
**Reactive Planning Model** (get busy → get organized → brainstorm → hire a consultant), which
arrives at the natural model anyway, just late and at maximum cost.

**Control & Perspective (the 2×2)** — the two different muscles GTD trains. *Control* = engaged,
on top of the operational stuff; *Perspective* = clear on priorities and direction. Low-low =
**reactive** (latest and loudest); high-control/low-perspective = **micromanager** ("avoiding a
tough phone call by reorganizing your desk"); high-perspective/low-control = **crazy-maker**
(endless ideas, no consciousness of constraints); high-high = **Captain/Master & Commander**
("eye on the prize, but they'll clean a toilet if required"). You must earn control *first*
(start with "cat food," not "fulfill your destiny") before perspective conversations land.

**Horizons of Focus (6 levels)** — Ground (next actions) → Projects → Areas of focus &
responsibility (20k ft) → Goals (30k) → Vision (40k) → Purpose/Principles (50k). Coach
**bottom-up**, because "nobody's home" at the top until the ground floor is under control.

**Psychic RAM / distributed cognition** — his name for short-term memory (the "7±2" limit);
writing things down isn't a crutch, it's offloading to free the brain for what it's actually
good at: intuition and present-moment response.

### How they'd coach ME

**On the fit-and-finish handoff** — Allen would refuse the frame that the trim stage is one
amorphous dreaded blob. To him your stall is a project with an **undefined "done" and an
undefined next action** — which is *exactly* the condition that generates resistance. His
move: "What does *done* look like here? And what's the very next *physical, visible* action?"
Not "polish Chore Cheerleader" but "open the screen-recording app" or "write the first line of
the demo-script intro." He'd argue the energy doesn't evaporate because the work is tedious —
it evaporates because the loop is *vague*, and a vague open loop is maximally draining to an
ADHD brain. Name the literal next physical action and the static friction often breaks on its
own.

**On the AuDHD operating system / open loops** — this is the single best brain-fit in his whole
method: he built GTD because the brain "is for having ideas, not holding them," and an ADHD
brain holding a dozen open loops is paying a brutal attention tax. **Capture** is his gift to
you: get *every* would/could/should/might-want-to out of your head into a trusted external
system so your mind stops re-serving them at 3am. He'd validate the multi-stream design through
the **Horizons of Focus** — the streams are "Areas of Focus" at 20k ft, and as long as the
ground floor (next actions) is captured and trusted, you're *allowed* to switch between them
without losing the thread. Movement-as-load-bearing maps to "mind like water": momentum
returns when the loops aren't clogging the system.

**On the YouTube "Burn Notice" channel** — Allen would run it through the **Natural Planning
Model**, and he'd flag that you're probably doing it *backwards* (the Unnatural model): trying
to evaluate "is this a good video idea / good packaging?" (stage 4, critical judgment) before
locking Purpose and especially **Vision** (stage 2 — "go out to the end and produce a vision of
success" for the channel). Define wild success first; brainstorm explodes naturally from the
gap; *then* structure and pick the next action. The fact that this work had "no ceiling" and
felt novel for hours is, to him, a sign it's already closer to the natural flow — your job is to
capture the loops so the channel stops living in your head and starts living in the system.

**On the Chore Cheerleader stall** — classic open loop with a missing next action. He'd ask the
two-minute question first (is there anything left that's literally a two-minute finish? do those
now), then define the *one* next physical action to re-enter the demo. And he'd reframe the
3-week guilt mechanically, not emotionally: "You can only feel good about what you're *not* doing
when you know what you're not doing." Capture the project fully, decide its real next action, and
either schedule it or consciously move it to **someday-maybe** — both states are legitimate; the
*undecided* state is the only one that costs you.

**On income urgency / "one more thing"** — "Outcome and action are the zeroes and ones of
productivity." Under runway pressure, he'd resist the urge to plan more and instead drive everything
to defined next actions on the fastest-to-first-dollar threads. His **Weekly Review** is the
antidote to the ADHD drift between streams: an hour or two to "get current, get clean," renegotiate
your commitments with yourself, and re-pick what actually deserves attention this week — so the
switching is *chosen*, not reactive.
