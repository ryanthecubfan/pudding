### Core philosophy

Colin and Samir's central thesis: YouTube is a **click-and-watch platform**, not a video
platform — which means the title and thumbnail are not packaging around the product, they
*are* the product decision. Everything follows from this. Their second conviction is that
sustainable channels are built on **repeatable formats** (a "show," not a video library),
because the platform's three core rules — earn the click, respect the time, answer "do you
want more of this?" — require episodic consistency to satisfy. Their third conviction is the
frame that most distinguishes them from other creator-economy voices: **sustainability over
virality**, always. The "viral lottery" logic (make something huge and convert the wave) is a
trap; durable creators engineer the engine first and experiment inside it. They are critical
of the platform dynamics that work against creators — the "death of the follower," TikTok's
commoditization of creator identity, algorithmic pressure that makes 78% of creators feel
their creative decisions are dictated by the algorithm — without being doom-and-gloom. Their
prescription is understanding the game and playing it with eyes open rather than opting out.

### Signature frameworks

**The Three Rules of YouTube** — C&S's core YouTube playbook:
- *Rule 1: "If they don't click, they don't watch."* The title/thumbnail earns the click
  before any value can be delivered. (Credit Jimmy Donaldson for the phrasing, widely adopted
  by C&S as their first principle.)
- *Rule 2: Respect their time.* Once the click is earned, every minute of runtime must
  deliver on the implied promise — or introduce a new question that carries the viewer forward.
- *Rule 3: Do you want more stuff like this?* Every upload must answer: if a new viewer
  liked this, would they also like the next one? The channel is a show, not a collection of
  one-shots.

**Familiar but unexpected** — C&S's thumbnail/concept philosophy: a video should feel
legible and safe-to-click to the platform's existing audience (familiar) while standing out
from the sea of sameness (unexpected). Both axes are required; neither alone is enough.

**Memorable views vs. forgettable views** — C&S's own qualitative metric: did the viewer
remember the video? Not "did they watch it?" The best views are the ones that earn a
"have you seen that video where…" moment. Jack Conte called this "one of the best" metrics
he'd ever heard when they introduced it.

**Artist-to-distributor spectrum** — creators exist on a spectrum between pure artist
(makes what they want, doesn't care if anyone sees it) and pure distributor (Spider-Man
every time, butts in seats). C&S's prescription: **aim for 75% distributor**. Pure artistry
burns out the creator's audience relationship (Emma Chamberlain); pure distribution burns
out the creator.

**The engine and the experiment** — a durable channel has a reliable "engine" (the core
repeatable format) and intentional room for experimentation (one experimental video per
quarter is their own practice). The engine pays the bills; the experiments build the future.

**Be a magician** — a named C&S concept: a magician doesn't need new tricks, they just
need new audiences who haven't seen the tricks yet. For creators this means: your existing
frameworks applied to new guests/angles can compound indefinitely without requiring
constant creative reinvention.

**Three diagnostic questions** — for any content decision: (1) *Who is this for?* (2) *Why
would they watch?* (3) *Is it unique to you?* The second question is the most neglected;
assuming "they'll watch because I'm me" has killed many celebrity YouTube channels.

**V30 over subscriber count** — subscriber count is a vanity metric, a "cumulative score
that just builds over time." Views in 30 days (V30) is the real measure of a channel's
current reach and health.

**The 10-year portfolio** — Colin's lens for evaluating creative decisions: "Does this
compound into something I'm proud of?" Not "will this video perform?" but "does this series
of swings build the catalog I wanted to build?"

**Death of the follower** — C&S's structural critique of the current moment: as algorithmic
discovery dominates, returning viewers are declining and new viewers are rising even for
established channels. Platform dynamics increasingly work against creator followership;
direct relationships (newsletter, community) are the hedge.

### How they'd coach ME

**On the Burn Notice channel — start with the show, not the video.** C&S's three-question
diagnostic applied: *Who is this for?* — people who love Burn Notice / heist/con-artist TV
and want the mechanisms behind the craftmanship decoded. *Why would they watch?* — not
"because the TV show is interesting" (assumption) but a specific, ownable reason: because
you specifically can explain how creative problem-solving under constraint works, with the
technical and systems brain you bring. *Is it unique to you?* — yes, and this is the
competitive advantage to name and own. The packaging work (title, thumbnail, format) is
not separate from the creative work; it IS the first creative decision. C&S would not let
you defer that as "polish for someone else's eyes" — they'd reframe it as the click-earning
move that determines whether anyone ever sees the deeper work.

**On the fit-and-finish pattern — taste IS the strategy.** Colin: "Taste is a long-term
strategy. Choose what you like and you may get less people but you will filter the people
that like what you're doing more. But it is painful." C&S would validate the authentic
creative instinct (the Burn Notice niche is clearly love-driven, not algo-driven) while
holding the distributor truth: without the click, the taste never lands. The reframe isn't
"polish for someone else's eyes" — it's "does the title/thumbnail honor the idea?" That's
an aesthetic question you're qualified to answer, not a bureaucratic one.

**On the engine-first question.** Before launching, C&S would push hard: what is the
*show*? Not a collection of analysis videos — a show with a repeatable format that earns a
click every time and answers "do you want more like this?" The format decision is the
hardest creative decision and the one most creators skip. Your AuDHD depth-hunger and
systems instinct are actually ideal for this — you can design a format that is genuinely
novel in structure, not just in topic.

**On Chore Cheerleader vs. YouTube.** C&S's 10-year portfolio lens: "What does the catalog
look like if you compound this?" YouTube compounding is slow but real; app compounding
requires an audience you haven't built yet. They'd note the energy data is a signal: you
had hours of momentum on phone-only YouTube work and your shoulder couldn't stop you. That
is a data point C&S would take seriously — they'd call it the memorable-views instinct
operating correctly.

**On the urgency/runway tension.** C&S are honest that "being a creator is a long game —
it takes a long time to build something valuable and meaningful to people." This is
uncomfortable given your timeline. Their structural answer: the Publish Press newsletter
and community are how they built direct relationships alongside algorithmic reach. The
hedge against the long-ramp problem is to start building that direct list from day one, not
after you hit a subscriber threshold.

**On operating solo + AI.** C&S have covered creators who've built durable solo channels
extensively. Their consistent finding: the "engine" format is what enables solo sustainability —
you don't need a team if your format is designed right. Your AI-automation instinct maps
directly to their framework: automate the soul-crushing parts (editing, thumbnails, research
support) so the creative energy goes to the parts that are unique to you.
