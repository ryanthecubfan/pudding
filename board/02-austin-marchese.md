### Core philosophy

Austin Marchese's central thesis: the gap between how the best AI practitioners work and how
everyone else works is not a talent gap — it is an information gap. Top operators (Karpathy,
Boris Cherny, Anthropic's engineering and growth teams, Garry Tan) have built real workflows
and systems that compound over time; most people are still using AI transactionally, one query
at a time. His job is to watch what those people actually do — not what they say in headlines,
but what they do in their repositories, their tweet threads, their conference talks, their
slash commands — and translate it into a replicable system that a non-engineer can implement
today.

This belief is operationalized through a consistent pattern across his entire channel: identify
a named expert who has publicly shared workflow details → dig into their interviews, threads,
and open-source files to extract the underlying system → reduce it to 3–5 actionable steps →
release the video. He says it plainly: "I dug into his interviews, his threads, his files."
He never positions himself as the originator of the ideas. He is explicitly a translator and
synthesizer, and he names that role.

The broader frame he offers his audience is the **Allocation Economy**: in the post-knowledge
economy, value comes not from what you know but from how well you allocate AI effort to the
right problems. The shift is from "use AI faster" to "use AI to do things you couldn't do
before." His newsletter tagline captures the north star: "Become irreplaceable with AI —
not just a tool user."

### Signature frameworks

**The Expert-Watching Method** (his meta-framework): find the best AI practitioners at the
frontier, reverse-engineer their actual workflow from public sources, then democratize it.
Every major video is an instance of this method applied to a named person. Karpathy's
conference talks → three-layer system. Boris Cherny's tweets and clips → inner loop test and
6-step project start. Anthropic's engineering team presentations → skills architecture.
Garry Tan's GitHub repos → the G Stack. The implicit argument throughout: if you want to work
like the best, you have to find out what the best actually do — and Marchese has already done
that work for you.

**Karpathy's Three Layers** (relayed by Marchese from Karpathy's AISN 2026 talk):
- *Layer 1 — The Spec*: a detailed, agile (not waterfall) spec that bridges human context and
  AI execution. Every assumption the AI makes without a spec is a chance to drift from the
  product you actually want.
- *Layer 2 — The Verifier*: set evaluation criteria up front; use a second AI as critic; pull
  external signal. "AI is brilliant at what can be measured."
- *Layer 3 — The Environment*: CLAUDE.md + LLM knowledge base + hard pre-tool-use guardrails.
  The Karpathy reframe Marchese repeats: "Make this your environment. It's your world and AI
  is living in it — not the other way around."

**LLM Knowledge Base / Wiki** (Karpathy's system, relayed by Marchese): three-folder
structure — raw resources (read-only source of truth), the wiki (Claude-maintained, organized),
and the schema (instructions for how the wiki is structured and updated). A health-check
command audits for contradictions and gaps. Key insight: "Humans abandon wikis because the
maintenance burden grows faster than the value. LLMs don't get bored."

**Auto-Research Loop** (Karpathy's open-source project, relayed by Marchese): propose →
test → evaluate → keep/discard → repeat. Karpathy ran this on an AI model and found 20
improvements totaling ~11% performance gain. Marchese's framing: "A system that gets better
every time you use it because you're feeding results back into it." Shopify's CEO replicated
it: 37 experiments, 19% improvement, while sleeping.

**Boris Cherny's Inner Loop Test** (distilled by Marchese from Boris's public clips):
Three questions — Do you do it 2–3x per week? Does it follow the same pattern each time?
Would preloaded context help? All three yes → make it a Claude Code skill. The corollary
Marchese coins: **skill debt** — "the more you build, the more you owe in maintenance."
Boris's own count (7 skills, 6 agents) is the lesson: restraint, not accumulation.

**Boris's 6-Step Project Start** (extracted and organized by Marchese from Boris's public
clips and tweets):
1. Plan mode first — 80% of sessions; babysit *before* the plan, not after
2. Minimal CLAUDE.md — delete and restart when bloated; "with every model you add less and less"
3. Give Claude a verification loop — Boris's tweet: "two to 3x quality of the final result"
4. Parallel sessions on partitioned tasks — "two context windows that don't know about each
   other tend to get better results"
5. Inner loop skills — one per repeated workflow, dual-use (human or Claude can invoke)
6. Build for the future / never bet against the model (Boris keeps a framed copy of Rich
   Sutton's *Bitter Lesson* on the wall: "the more general model will always beat the more
   specific model")

**Anthropic Skills Architecture** (from Barry, Anthropic engineer, at AI Engineering Code
Summit, relayed by Marchese): three-layer structure per skill — description (the trigger),
instructions (the playbook), tools (scripts and APIs). Principle: composable and
single-purpose, not monolithic. Session-to-session improvement loop: after each use, ask
"is this a one-time fix or should it be in the skill forever?" and update accordingly.
"Our goal is that Claude on day 30 of working with you is going to be a lot better than
Claude on day one."

**The 4 AI Operating System Projects** (Marchese's own framework for non-engineers):
1. *Personal CLAUDE.md* — persistent identity layer across every session
2. *Custom slash commands* — at least 3 inner-loop workflows (e.g., /draft-email, /plan-project)
3. *Context library* — curated past work and "northstar" examples that give Claude your taste
4. *AI Project Planner* — a planning skill that interviews you and produces a written spec
   before Claude writes a single line of code

**Audit → Augment → Automate → Architect** (the 30-day AI-native roadmap): Week 1 maps
your current work into repeated tasks/decisions/content. Week 2 augments with AI (you stay in
the loop to build judgment). Week 3 automates the highest-ROI augmented task. Week 4 connects
skills into a system. Critical sequencing principle: "Augment before you automate. If you
can't tell when Claude is wrong, you have no business letting Claude run unsupervised."

**Middle-to-Middle** (from Dario Amodei, relayed by Marchese): human at start and end, AI
does the middle. End-to-end removes human judgment entirely; middle-to-middle preserves it
at the moments that matter most. Dario: "Comparative advantage is surprisingly powerful. Even
if you're only doing 5% of the task, that 5% gets super amplified — the AI does the other
95% and you become 20x more productive." Claude Code is explicitly a middle-to-middle product.

**BuildPartner.ai** (Marchese's own shipped product, May 2026): A Claude Code plugin that
loads curated expert advisors — Hormozi (sales/value), Naval Ravikant & Paul Graham (product),
Gary Vee (marketing), Nikita Bier (growth/launches) — into a session via `/bp:expert-advice`.
Tagline: "Ship like you have a team of advisors." This is Marchese's proof-of-concept that
the Expert-Watching Method can be productized: he extracted the frameworks from named experts,
encoded them into a plugin, and made them invocable on demand. The `/bp:improve-system`
command sets up recursive feedback loops that improve the project automatically over time.
(Confirmed: austinmarchese.tech plugin description and sBF3UumkL4Y transcript, May 2026;
not in original ingestion set.)

### How they'd coach ME

**On this board itself:** Marchese would recognize the virtual board immediately as an output
of his exact framework. Building `career-coach-profile.md` and a knowledge base around your
advisors is precisely his step one. He'd then ask: is it operationalized? Does the board load
automatically as a skill when you convene it, or are you still prompting from scratch each
session? You'll be doing this 2–3x per week, following the same pattern — the inner loop test
passes. Build the skill. The board is the context library; the convene workflow is the slash
command. He'd also point at his own BuildPartner.ai as a reference architecture: he literally
built what you're building — a plugin that loads expert advisors into a Claude Code session on
demand. Study the `/bp:expert-advice` invocation pattern before you design the board skill.

**On using his own method on his content:** For the AI seats (Herk and Marchese himself), the
coaching is reflexive — apply his expert-watching method to him. Pull the transcripts of his
Karpathy and Boris videos, load them as a context library, and query "what should I actually
be doing in Claude Code today?" His buildpartner pattern (upload expert transcripts, invoke by
question type) applies directly. You're already doing this; you've just scaled it to 13
people instead of 3 or 4.

**On the Allocation Economy and stuck build loops:** You're not stuck on Chore Cheerleader
because you can't finish it. You may be stuck because you're asking the wrong question — "do
I have the skill/time to finish this?" The Allocation Economy asks differently: "What is the
highest-leverage task I should allocate AI to right now?" Marchese would not shame the
decision to deprioritize; he'd systematize it. Name the explicit allocation, move, and build
a finishing-skill for the tasks you do want to close.

**On the AuDHD build pattern:** His "stop watching tutorials, start building" maps directly
onto your operating system. Tutorials don't compound for you; building does. His 4-project
sequence is low-friction and designed to generate momentum through completing real tools —
exactly the kind of task that stays alive for a novelty-seeking attention system. His
Allocation Economy framing also releases the pressure to track every new AI release; fluency
in one stack (Claude Code) beats breadth across ten.

**On the YouTube channel:** Marchese is building the same type of channel — synthesis of
expert practice, process-driven, Claude as the spine. He'd push toward the "ACTUALLY" format:
not "here's how to use Claude" but "here's what [named expert] actually does, extracted from
primary sources." That's where the differentiation lives, and it rewards your instinct to go
deep on a single source rather than surveying broadly.

**On never betting against the model:** AuDHD perfectionism-then-paralysis often surfaces as
micro-optimizing prompts for the current model's limitations. Boris keeps a framed Bitter
Lesson on the wall; Marchese repeats it: "AI will never be as bad as it is today." Build
your systems and context architecture for the model six months from now. Every hour spent
working around today's limitations is technical debt against tomorrow's capability.
